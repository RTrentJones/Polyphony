BORED TO UNDEATH — a synopsis

PART ONE: THE FIRM

Death, it turns out, is not an ending but an onboarding.

When the world's largest afterlife-services conglomerate, Aeon Holdings, quietly
won the exclusive concession to process the recently deceased three centuries
ago, no one living noticed, because no one living was the customer. The customers
arrived already dead, disoriented, and — crucially — in no position to read the
terms of service. Aeon's founding insight was elegant and terrible: a soul, freed
from the friction of a body, is the most efficient labor unit ever devised. It
does not sleep. It does not unionize, historically speaking. It does not age out
of usefulness, because it has already aged out of everything. It simply needs to
be told what to do, and given the faint, renewable hope that doing it well might
one day earn a promotion out of the ledger and into whatever lies beyond the
beyond.

That hope is the product. That hope is the whole business.

Aeon calls its core operation the Undeath Pipeline, and in the internal style
guide the term is always capitalized, always singular, and never — under any
circumstances — described in plain language to a new hire. The Pipeline is the
machinery by which raw grief is refined into shareholder value: intake,
orientation, assignment, extraction, and, for the vanishingly few, "graduation."
Most of the dead never learn what the Pipeline actually pipes, or where. They
learn their department. They learn their quota. They learn the difference between
a billable haunting and a non-billable one, and they learn not to ask what
happens to the souls whose contracts are "restructured."

The afterlife economy runs on a single currency, called tenure, which is accrued
by labor and spent on the only thing the dead still want: the possibility of
moving on. You cannot buy tenure. You cannot steal it, though people try. You can
only earn it, quarter by quarter, in the gray fluorescent light of the Marrow
District, the vast subterranean office park where the Pipeline's clerical work is
done. The Marrow District has no windows, because there is nothing outside it to
see, and no clocks, because time is a benefit reserved for management.

There is a culture to the place, and the culture is the cruelest technology Aeon
ever built. New hires are given a lanyard, a desk, and a mentor, and the mentor is
always someone three quarters of the way to burning out, because a soul who still
believes is a poor teacher of resignation. There are all-hands meetings, held in
an amphitheater carved from a single slab of the bedrock beneath the Marrow
District, at which the quarter's graduation — always exactly one, always announced
with confetti that dissolves before it lands — is celebrated as proof that the
system works. There are performance reviews, calibrated on a curve so that
someone must always be failing, so that the fear is evenly distributed and never
pools into anything as dangerous as solidarity. There are team-building
exercises. There is a newsletter. The newsletter is the worst part, and everyone
agrees, quietly, at the coffee station that dispenses a gray liquid the dead drink
out of habit rather than need, that the newsletter is the worst part, and no one
ever says so where it could be documented.

The departments spread out from the Pipeline like rings of a felled tree.
Intake greets the newly dead and sorts them by exploitable virtue. Orientation
teaches them the vocabulary that will keep them from ever describing their
situation accurately. Assignment matches a soul's dearest surviving habit to the
task that will grind it down most efficiently: the meticulous go to Records, the
loyal go to Retention, the curious — the dangerous, curious ones — go to
Reconciliations, where curiosity is spent closing the accounts of the disappeared
until there is none left. Above them all floats Management, a stratum of the dead
so thoroughly graduated-in-spirit that they no longer remember wanting to leave,
and above Management, alone, the office at the top.

At the very top of Aeon Holdings sits an office that no employee has ever
entered and returned from unchanged. The being who occupies it holds a title so
old that it has worn down, through centuries of memos and org charts and
mandatory compliance trainings, into a three-letter abbreviation that new hires
mistake, understandably, for the name of a subsidiary or a line of business. They
hear "the CEL" and they picture a department — Corporate something, Logistics
something — and they are not corrected, because the misunderstanding is useful.
The CEL is not a department. The CEL is a person, or was one, a very long time
ago. The letters stand for Chief Executive Lich, and the Lich is real, and the
Lich is watching the numbers.

This is a story about three of the dead, and about what happens to a machine
built on hope when the people inside it finally, quietly, stop hoping.

PART TWO: THE NEW HIRE

Milo Voss died the way most people do: unremarkably, mid-sentence, on a Tuesday,
while complaining about a meeting that could have been an email. In life he had
been a mid-level operations analyst for a company that made a component that went
into a component that went into something no one could name, and he had been, by
his own cheerful admission, extraordinarily good at a job that did not matter. He
had spent forty-one years optimizing processes whose purpose he never questioned,
and he had been comfortable, and he had been bored, and the boredom had felt, at
the time, like a personality.

So when Milo Voss opened his eyes in the intake atrium of the Undeath Pipeline
and a caseworker slid a laminated org chart across a laminate desk and explained,
in the warm and terrible voice of onboarding everywhere, that he had been
retained, that his skills were a strong culture fit, and that he would be
starting Monday in Reconciliations — Milo felt something he had not felt in
decades. He felt seen. He felt, God help him, useful. The afterlife had looked at
his forty-one years of pointless optimization and decided they were an asset, and
Milo Voss, who had never in his life or death been told that his boredom was
valuable, said yes before anyone thought to offer him a choice.

It takes him the better part of a fiscal year to understand what he has agreed
to. Reconciliations, he learns, is the department that closes the accounts of
souls whose contracts have been restructured — that reviews their remaining
tenure, zeroes it out, and files the paperwork that sends them not onward, but
under, into the part of the Pipeline that the style guide will not name. Milo has
been hired, on the strength of his gift for not asking questions, to be very good
at the one job in the afterlife that ought to require asking every question there
is. And he is good at it. That is the horror of the middle of this book: Milo Voss
is good at it, and he likes being good at it, and the liking lasts almost until
the end.

PART THREE: THE ORGANIZER

Zara Okafor has been dead for two hundred and six years, and she has spent almost
all of them angry.

She died young, in a factory fire that the living world found regrettable and the
Pipeline found routine, and she arrived in the Marrow District with the one trait
Aeon Holdings has never found a way to refine out of a soul: she could not stop
noticing who was getting hurt. Two centuries of noticing have made her the most
dangerous kind of employee — the kind who has read the entire contract, including
the appendices, including the parts printed in the ink that only shows up under
grief. Zara knows what the Pipeline pipes. She has known for a hundred years. What
she has never had is leverage, because a soul alone has none, and the dead, Aeon
has made very sure, are always alone.

Zara Okafor is Milo's desk neighbor. She is assigned to train him, and she does,
flawlessly, with a flat professional courtesy that Milo mistakes for friendship
and then, slowly, comes to understand is something far more patient and far more
frightening: recruitment. Zara has spent decades identifying the one structural
weakness in the Undeath Pipeline, and it is not a vault or a password or a
weapon. It is a job. It is Reconciliations. Whoever controls the department that
closes the accounts controls the one ledger the Lich actually reads. And Zara has
just been handed a brand-new Reconciliations analyst who is lonely, and gifted,
and bored, and who has no idea yet that he is the most important soul in the
building.

PART FOUR: THE LICH IN THE CORNER OFFICE

Edric Thane was the third son of a minor merchant house, and he was, in life, a
clerk. This is the joke the whole novel is built on and the reason it aches: the
most powerful being in the afterlife, the Chief Executive Lich, the CEL whose
initials terrify every new hire, was once exactly what Milo Voss is now. Edric
Thane died six hundred years ago owing a debt he could not pay, and he was
retained, and he was good at it, and he was ambitious in the specific, grinding,
upward way of a man who has decided that the only escape from a machine is to
own it. He climbed. He reconciled. He restructured. He learned that tenure could
be pooled, and that a soul who controlled enough pooled tenure could purchase
something no clerk was ever meant to have: continuance. Edric Thane did not
graduate out of the Pipeline. He devoured it, and wore it, and became the reason
no one else ever would.

Now he sits at the top of Aeon Holdings, neither living nor properly dead, a
clerk's resentment scaled up to the size of an institution, and he is not cruel
so much as he is thorough. Edric Thane believes, with the total conviction of the
converted, that hope is a renewable resource and that spending it is what it is
for. He is the villain of this book, and he is right about almost everything, and
that is what makes him terrible: he was Milo once. He was Zara's colleague once.
He is what the Pipeline is designed to produce, the one graduate it will permit,
and his continued existence is the proof, filed and notarized, that the way out
is real — for exactly one soul, and never again.

PART FIVE: THE RESTRUCTURING

The plot turns on a routine account. Milo Voss, closing the books on a soul whose
contract has been restructured, notices — because Zara Okafor has spent a year
teaching him, gently, to notice — that the numbers do not reconcile. Tenure is
disappearing from the ledger faster than labor can explain, and it is all flowing
to a single pooled account at the top of the org chart. Milo has found, without
looking for it, the mechanism by which the CEL sustains himself: every soul
"sent under" is not destroyed but harvested, their accrued hope funneled upward to
keep one ancient clerk continuing. The Undeath Pipeline is not a business with a
monstrous side effect. The monstrous side effect is the business. Everything else
is overhead.

What Milo does with this knowledge is the spine of the back half of the book. His
first instinct — his forty-one years of instinct, his six-hundred-years-ago
Edric Thane instinct — is to optimize it. To report the discrepancy, to be
useful, to be seen, to climb. Zara lets him sit with that instinct until he
cannot stand himself, and then she offers him the alternative she has spent two
centuries building toward: not a rebellion, not a war, nothing so loud that the
Lich could file it under threat and restructure it away. A work stoppage. A
union of the dead. The one thing a machine that runs on hope cannot survive is
the coordinated, documented, procedurally impeccable refusal of its workforce to
keep hoping on schedule.

They do it the only way that could possibly work: as paperwork. Zara has the
organizing; Milo has the ledger; and between them they turn the CEL's own
bureaucracy into the instrument of his undoing, filing the restructuring of Edric
Thane's account through the very department built to close accounts. The climax is
not a battle. It is a quarterly review. Milo Voss stands in the one office no
employee returns from unchanged and hands the Chief Executive Lich a single sheet
of paper — a resignation, his own, and attached to it, procedurally flawless, the
reconciliation of every soul the Pipeline ever sent under, tenure restored, hope
returned, the pooled account that keeps Edric Thane continuing zeroed out by the
same rules Edric spent six centuries enforcing.

Edric Thane, who was a clerk, is undone by a clerk, according to the terms of
service, and the terrible mercy of the ending is that he understands it
completely, and files it correctly, and goes wherever it is the dead go when they
are finally, at last, allowed to be bored no longer.

The dead do not overthrow Aeon Holdings. That would be a different, easier book.
They do something quieter and harder: they read the contract to the end, together,
and hold the machine to its own word. Milo Voss gets his boredom back, and learns
it was never a personality — it was a wound where his questions used to be. Zara
Okafor gets the one thing two centuries of anger never could: company. And the
Undeath Pipeline, for the first time in three hundred years, has to explain to
its customers, in plain language, exactly what it is that it does.

PART SIX: THE PEOPLE IN THE MARGINS

The book is populated, in its middle chapters, by the small and specific dead who
give the satire its ache. There is Prava, the intake caseworker who onboarded
Milo with such practiced warmth and who, it emerges, has onboarded eleven thousand
souls and remembers the name of every one, and keeps them in a private ledger she
is not permitted to have, and weeps, silently and off the clock, at each
graduation because it is the only proof she has that leaving is possible. There is
Old Fen, who has been in Records so long that he has begun to file himself, whose
tragedy is that he is content, whose contentment is the thing Zara most fears and
Milo most envies. There is the Retention team, cheerful and doomed, whose job is
to talk the wavering out of despair and back onto the schedule, and who are very
good at it, and who are, without exception, the most despairing souls in the
building.

And there is the question the novel keeps in its pocket and never quite lets the
reader forget: what, exactly, is on the other side of graduation? Aeon promises an
"onward." The style guide will not define it. Edric Thane, the one soul to ever
buy his way past the ledger, chose not to take it — chose continuance in the
Marrow District over whatever the onward is — and that choice is the crack in the
Lich the whole plot slides a lever into. Because if graduation were truly the
reward the Pipeline sells, the most powerful being in the afterlife would have
taken it six hundred years ago. Edric Thane stayed. Edric Thane is afraid of the
onward. And a machine whose owner does not believe its own product is a machine
that can be brought down by anyone willing to read that fact out loud.

THEMES AND TEXTURE

At its heart, Bored to Undeath is a workplace comedy that curdles, by degrees,
into a tragedy about complicity — about how the most efficient evil is not the
kind that requires monsters but the kind that requires only competent, tired,
reasonable people who have agreed not to ask the next question. Milo Voss is not a
chosen one; he is a good employee, and the book's argument is that in a system
like the Pipeline those are the same thing until the moment they are not. Zara
Okafor is not a warrior; she is an organizer, and her weapon is not a sword but a
signature sheet, and the book insists, page after page, that this is the more
frightening thing to be, because a sword threatens the Lich and a signature sheet
threatens the terms of service, and the Lich can survive a threat but not a
correctly filed contradiction.

The tone is dry, deadpan, and specific: the horror is administrative, delivered in
the language of quarterly targets and culture fit and "restructuring," and the
comedy comes from the exact, lovingly observed gap between what the Pipeline says
it does and what it does. The undead here do not moan; they attend meetings. The
Lich does not roar; he reviews. The apocalypse, when it comes, comes as paperwork,
and the revolution, when it comes, comes as a work stoppage announced by an
all-staff email that is, for once, completely honest.

The setting — Aeon Holdings, the Undeath Pipeline, the windowless Marrow District,
the currency of tenure, the graduation that may be salvation or may be a nicer
word for the same disappearance — is the novel's central invention and its central
metaphor, and none of it should be flattened into a generic corporate dystopia,
because the specific joke is theological: this is what you get when an institution
designed to shepherd souls decides, quite reasonably, quite gradually, that the
souls are the inventory. The CEL is not a brand. The Pipeline is not a factory.
Milo and Zara and Edric are not archetypes wearing office clothes. They are three
clerks, six centuries apart, discovering the same terrible fork in the same gray
hallway, and choosing, two of them, at last, the harder turn.
